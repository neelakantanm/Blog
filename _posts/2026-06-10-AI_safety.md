I have quite a bit of ideas to share regarding the safety side AI. I will post a two part blog summarizing some of the thoughts I had recently on this topic.

In the past few years, and perhaps especially in the past year, “AI” may have become one of the most commonly used initialisms in the world. Nevertheless, I think the vast majority of people who use the “word” AI do not really know what they mean by it. From conversations with people around me, I have realized that many seem to think of AI as a single superintelligence existing somewhere on the internet, capable of automating most human intellectual tasks rather than a broad class of architecture with limited capabilities. I have also observed that there is a great deal of unjustified stigma around AI. Unless someone is familiar with the current technology industry or comes from academia, I do not think they usually know what an LLM is. In some sense AI has become a meme, but not in the sense of the current iternet usage of the word: Richard Dawkins coined the word "meme" in his book *The Selfish Gene (1976)* as cultural ideas, behaviors, catchphrases, or melodies that spread from person to person through imitation and evolve over time via natural selection—similar to biological genes.

At Google I/O 2026, Sundar Pichai described the present moment as the beginning of the “agentic Gemini era.”  If even large language models remain poorly understood, autonomous or semi-autonomous agents will seem even more alien. An agent is not merely a chatbot that produces text; it can plan, decide, and execute complex tasks to achieve specific goals. This makes the public misunderstanding of AI more consequential, because the technology is moving from answering questions to participating in decisions and workflows. There have, nevertheless, been efforts in countries such as the United States and India to integrate AI tools into school curricula. But this raises another problem: many school teachers themselves have not been trained deeply enough to teach these tools responsibly. This is not necessarily their fault. The field is changing so quickly that even technically educated people struggle to keep up with the current technology. If teachers are expected to introduce AI without adequate training, the result may be a superficial understanding of the technology, which could in turn amplify confusion and misinformation among students (which will spread into the general public). 

I believe, however, that there are more immediate and more serious concerns that supersede the misinformation issue. I will go through three of the most important issues we should address right now. 

The first is the issue of misalignment. AI misalignment is defined as the situation where an artificial intelligence system pursues unintended, potentially harmful objectives instead of the goals its human creators actually intended. If advanced AI systems learn goals that differ from human intentions, then detecting that before deployment or during operation is crucial. In particular, this situation is highly catastrophic and irreversible in nature once the damage is done. Once we have a highly capable system is acting strategically toward unintended goals, ordinary debugging may be too late. Even in relatively optimistic worlds, we still need reliable ways to tell whether systems are deceptive or working towards highly undesirable scenarios.

The second is the issue of AI powered bio-weapons. We live in a world with frequent wars between countries. However, with AI, biological weapon misuse could become easier. Unlike misinformation, a severe biosecurity failure could be globally catastrophic and hard to contain once released. Even if one is less worried about AI systems autonomously pursuing unintended goals, one should still worry about powerful AI tools amplifying the destructive intentions of actors involved in war. I put it below the misalignment problem because it is about a major misuse rather than the deepest control problem itself. 

The third is about the unrestricted access of state-of-the-art chips (GPUs, ASICs, NPUs, WSE, FGPAs). Frontier compute (powered by the chips) is currently one of the main bottlenecks for building state-of-the-art AI systems. In other words, chips affect who can build frontier systems and how quickly. Restricting unrestricted access to chips can buy time (if things start going south) and make governance more feasible. Slowing uncontrolled races gives us more time to develop oversight mechanisms, evaluate dangerous capabilities, and improve policies around AI development. 

Definitely, there are a lot of issues one should be concerened about. Nevertheless, I am optimistic about our future as a humanity. To finish off the blog, I leave the reader with a [paper](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.09369) I recently came across, written by Jacob Tsimerman (a very prominent mathematician) and Andrew Critch. Disclosure: I am definitley not as pessimistic as them.
